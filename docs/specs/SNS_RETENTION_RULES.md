# SNS and Viewer-Retention Rules for AIPPT Decks

Status: draft v0.1  
Date: 2026-05-19

## 1. Core Principle

AIPPT is used in front of people: recording, teaching, training, and public explanation. A good slide is not just beautiful. It must help viewers keep watching and help the creator extract short clips, screenshots, and thumbnails.

The operating rule:

> Every slide should either advance understanding, create tension, prove value, or produce a reusable media moment.

## 2. The 3-30-300 Rule

Each deck should be designed for three viewing horizons:

- 3 seconds: the viewer understands why this page matters.
- 30 seconds: the speaker can explain the page without the audience getting lost.
- 300 seconds: the deck section has enough structure to become a short course segment.

If a page cannot work at 3 seconds, its title or visual focus is too weak. If it cannot work at 30 seconds, it lacks structure. If it cannot work inside a 300-second segment, it may be decorative rather than useful.

## 3. Slide-Level Retention Checklist

Each slide must answer:

- What is the one claim?
- Where should the eye land first?
- What should appear second?
- What word or visual should the audience remember?
- Can this page become a short-video screenshot?

Required page ingredients:

- Claim: one clear sentence.
- Contrast: before/after, problem/solution, old/new, manual/AI, scattered/systematic.
- Focus: one highlighted element.
- Progress: where this slide sits in the talk.
- Speaker runway: enough structure for the creator to talk naturally.

## 4. SNS Candidate Standards

Mark 25-35% of slides as SNS candidates.

A candidate slide should include:

- One strong sentence that can stand alone.
- Large type visible on mobile.
- No dependency on the previous slide.
- One obvious visual metaphor, diagram, contrast, or result.
- Enough empty space for cropping.
- A screenshot or thumbnail export path.

Bad SNS candidate:

- Dense table.
- Four equal cards with no focus.
- A slide that only makes sense after three previous slides.
- A page full of small prompt text.

Good SNS candidate:

- "公司不是缺 AI，而是缺一套能复用的 AI 工作流"
- "新人上手从 2 周变成 2 天，不靠天才员工，靠标准系统"
- "普通聊天是临时助理，Project 是公司资料室，GPT 是岗位员工"

## 5. Narrative Hooks

Use hook types deliberately:

- Contradiction hook: "你以为在买工具，其实在搭系统。"
- Pain hook: "老板越能干，公司越离不开老板。"
- Result hook: "把口头经验变成每个人都能执行的流程。"
- Demonstration hook: "这一页直接展示 AI 怎么接管重复工作。"
- Mistake hook: "多数公司用 AI 失败，不是因为模型差。"
- Identity hook: "这不是给技术人员的课，是给老板和管理者的系统课。"

Do not use fake suspense if the slide cannot immediately pay it off.

## 6. Reading Path Patterns

Use one of these patterns per slide:

- Z path: title top-left or top-center, content scans left to right, conclusion bottom-right.
- Spotlight path: one central object receives glow or scale emphasis.
- Ladder path: numbered steps guide vertical reading.
- Split contrast: left bad/right good, old/new, before/after.
- Orbit path: one central concept surrounded by supporting pieces.
- Reveal path: idea unfolds in stages for live teaching.

The editor should let users add visual guides: arrow, focus ring, glow line, step chip, highlight bar, and dimmed background.

## 7. Motion Rules for Retention

Motion should answer "where do I look next?"

Allowed motion:

- Fade in title.
- Stagger cards in reading order.
- Glow the important card after all cards appear.
- Draw a line along a process path.
- Pulse one metric or keyword once.
- Dim old state before showing new state.

Avoid:

- All cards moving at the same time.
- Infinite bouncing text.
- Particle effects over body copy.
- Slide transitions that hide the structure for too long.
- Motion that makes screenshots look blurry or mid-transition.

## 8. Visual Density Rules

Public course:

- 35-65 visible words per slide.
- Strong title and diagram/card structure.
- 1-2 pages per concept.

Employee training:

- 55-95 visible words per slide.
- More procedural clarity, fewer decorative effects.
- Checklists and forbidden actions are acceptable.

Self-media filming:

- 15-45 visible words per slide.
- Bigger text and stronger screenshot value.
- Keep SNS candidates frequent.

Product or service pitch:

- 25-60 visible words per slide.
- Strong before/after proof.
- Clear CTA and trust signals.

## 9. Thumbnail and Cropping Guidance

For long-form YouTube-style publishing, export a 16:9 thumbnail candidate from the deck. Use a high-resolution 16:9 export and keep the main subject readable at small sizes.

For vertical platforms, design SNS candidate pages so the central claim survives a 9:16 crop. If a slide depends on a wide table, it is not a vertical candidate.

For feed/carousel use, create a 1:1 crop preview. Square crops should preserve the title, the main visual, and one proof or action line.

## 10. External Platform Evidence Used

The rules above use current platform guidance as design constraints, not as the whole product strategy:

- TikTok for Business recommends vertical 9:16, at least 720P, content inside UI safe zones, a hook in the first 6 seconds, content proposition in the first 3 seconds, captions/text overlays, transitions, and a clear CTA. Source: <https://ads.tiktok.com/help/article/creative-best-practices>
- YouTube Help recommends custom thumbnails use a 16:9 aspect ratio and high resolution; YouTube's title/thumbnail A/B testing optimizes winners by watch-time share rather than click-through alone. Sources: <https://support.google.com/youtube/answer/72431> and <https://support.google.com/youtube/answer/16391400>
- Meta's Reels business guidance emphasizes 9:16 video, audio, and key messages inside the safe zone. Source: <https://www.facebook.com/business/ads/facebook-instagram-reels-ads>

## 11. Deck-Level Acceptance

Before a deck is considered good enough:

- At least 4 of 16 slides are SNS candidates.
- The first 3 slides make the audience understand why they should keep watching.
- Every 4-slide segment contains at least one contrast, proof, or demo.
- No two adjacent slides use the same visual rhythm unless intentionally forming a sequence.
- The final slide gives a next action that can be read in 3 seconds.
